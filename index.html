THE CONSTANT

The future creates new industries.
Not new psychology.

Every failure begins with something accepted without examination: an assumption about human behaviour, a story that seemed true until reality contradicted it, a condition that existed when the decision was made but vanished when the consequences arrived. The most dangerous claims are the ones nobody is willing to question, because the moment someone does, the whole structure shows how little it was built on.

How a $5 billion satellite network failed on an assumption nobody tested

$0

committed to a satellite network

1990

Motorola commits $5 billion and ten years to a network of 66 low-earth-orbit satellites. Nobody wrote it down.

Ten years of runway. The claim goes unwritten.
The assumption

Customers who need to communicate from remote locations will pay a significant premium to do so.

Valid in 1990

Sound. Supported. Widely agreed. The problem wasn't the assumption.

The condition underneath it

Terrestrial mobile had to remain unavailable in the target markets at the point of launch.

Gone by 1998
1998

Technically flawless. Bankrupt eleven months later.

The assumption didn't fail. The condition it depended on did.
11 months to bankruptcy
$5B written off
What this would have surfaced before launch

The claim holds only while terrestrial mobile coverage stays unavailable in target markets at launch. That condition had already ended three premium-communications bets when ground infrastructure outpaced the project timeline.

Condition identified Terrestrial mobile expansion rate vs. project timeline
Precedent examined Three premium-communications failures, 1970–1989, same failure mode
Conclusion Assumption valid in 1990. Condition no longer holds at projected 1998 launch. Proceed only with revised timeline or differentiated customer segment.

The core discipline

Human behaviour

The baseline every durable strategy has to satisfy. People respond to incentives, social pressure, and switching costs in ways that are remarkably stable across contexts. When people don't behave the way the strategy needed them to, everything built on that expectation collapses with it.

human
behaviour
02 / 05
Supporting check

Story

The story usually holds right up until it doesn't. The difference between what the story promises and what the incentive structure actually rewards is where trust collapses, and where the internal room stops being honest with itself.

story
03 / 05
Supporting check

Signals

Shifts that matter rarely announce themselves. They appear first as weak data points most teams aren't watching for. By the time they're visible to everyone, the window to respond has closed.

signals
04 / 05
Supporting check

Incentives

The stated goal and the actual reward structure point in different directions more often than anyone admits. When they diverge, the reward structure wins. Quietly. From inside the organisation that built both.

incentives

Execution

How it works

Five stages.
One named conclusion.

Retrodictive Validation™: name the assumption, test it against precedent.

Stage 01 of 05
01

Name the assumption

Before anything else, we find the one claim your decision depends on and write it in a single sentence.

"What exactly has to be true?"
02

Build the reference set

We examine every relevant case across sectors, periods, and markets where this claim has been tested under real conditions. We examine the full set, verify relevance, and document the base rate: how often this claim held, and where.

"Where else has this exact bet been made?"
03

Identify the conditions

Claims that hold everywhere are rare. Most hold under specific conditions and collapse when those conditions shift. We name them. Most internal reviews skip this step because it means admitting the conditions might already be gone.

"What had to be true for this to work?"
04

Trace the failures

For every case where the claim broke, we trace the condition that flipped the outcome. This is where the conclusion earns its weight. A pattern across failures is a finding. One data point is a story. We only return conclusions the evidence earns.

"Where, specifically, did this stop holding?"
05

Deliver your Decision Record

A written assessment: the claim stated plainly, the cases it's built on, the confidence level and what drives it, and the one condition that changes the conclusion. Where stakeholders are part of the picture, you also get a working map of who moves and what shifts them, not a slide that lives in a folder no one opens. You keep it. We have no further stake in what you decide.

"What would have to be false for us to be wrong?"
